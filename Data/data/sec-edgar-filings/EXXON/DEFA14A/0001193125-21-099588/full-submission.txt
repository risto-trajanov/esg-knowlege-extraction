<TD VALIGN="top" ALIGN="center"><B>ENERGY FACTOR PERSPECTIVES POST</B></TD></TR>
</TABLE> <P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Why ExxonMobil Supports Carbon Pricing </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">By Darren Woods </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">March&nbsp;29, 2021 </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">The recent steps by the American Petroleum Institute (API) to support a carbon price will contribute to advancing a lower-carbon future. For some time, we have
been encouraging trade associations to support a price on carbon and promote actions that enable the goals of the Paris Agreement. We encourage Congress to adopt this market-based, national policy solution. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">To better understand the steps needed to help realize a lower-carbon future, it&#146;s helpful to begin with some important context: the world&#146;s growing
need for energy. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Underpinning future energy demand is economic growth driven by an increasing population and growing prosperity. The continuing demand for
our products is concentrated in three growing sectors: power generation, industrial, and commercial transportation. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Today, there is a lack of
lower-emission alternatives to adequately meet the many needs in these sectors, which together account for 80&nbsp;percent of global energy-related CO2 emissions. We have spent decades researching new technologies to address this challenge, and
recently created a new business, ExxonMobil Low Carbon Solutions, to commercialize key technologies from our extensive research and development portfolio. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">For example, ExxonMobil Low Carbon Solutions is advancing plans for more than 20 new carbon capture and storage (CCS) opportunities around the world to enable
large-scale emission reductions, building upon a Carbon Capture Venture we began in 2018. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">We&#146;re the global leader in carbon capture and have
captured more than 40&nbsp;percent of all the world&#146;s captured anthropogenic CO2. ExxonMobil was the first company in the world to capture more than 120&nbsp;million tonnes of CO2, equivalent to the annual emissions of more than 25&nbsp;million
cars. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">We&#146;ll also leverage ExxonMobil&#146;s significant experience in the production of hydrogen which, when coupled with CCS, is likely to play a
critical role in a lower-carbon energy system. Other <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">low-carbon</FONT> technology focus areas in our portfolio will be added as they mature to commercialization. We&#146;re planning to invest $3&nbsp;billion on
lower-emission energy solutions through 2025 on top of $10&nbsp;billion we&#146;ve spent over the past two decades. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">In other emission-reduction efforts,
we are working to find new and better ways to monitor and reduce methane emissions, including via a collaboration with universities, environmental groups, and other industry partners. In 2019, we reduced our methane emissions nearly 18&nbsp;percent
across our U.S. unconventional operations, compared to 2016, when the Paris Agreement was signed. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Overall, our operated greenhouse gas emissions went
down by 6&nbsp;percent from 2016 to 2019, and we&#146;ve laid out plans for further reductions by 2025. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">While we will continue to advance these plans,
there is also a clear role for government&#151;which brings us back to policy. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Durable, predictable, and cost-effective policies will be required to
develop and deploy multiple needed <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">low-carbon</FONT> technologies at scale. Carbon pricing would send a clear signal through the market, creating incentives to reduce emissions. Such a signal will also incentivize
and coordinate the research, investment, and technology development that is needed to bring about key solutions. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Putting a price on carbon will allow
policymakers to eliminate the inefficient patchwork of regulations that is broadly recognized to be more expensive. Through the current approach, policymakers are putting a very high, but hidden, price on carbon that people can&#146;t see and are
unaware they are paying. An explicit price on carbon would be transparent, incentivize behavior to reduce emissions, allow the market to function efficiently, and stimulate the cross-sector opportunities needed to uncover the largest emission
reduction opportunities at the lowest cost. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">API now joins major trade groups representing diverse industries in publicly backing carbon pricing.
Collectively, they echo a report released last August in which the International Energy Agency noted, &#147;Carbon pricing, in particular, is a useful tool to guide investment decisions, especially those that will have long-term impacts on future
emissions.&#148; </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">The Climate Leadership Council (CLC), of which ExxonMobil is a founding member, is similarly calling for the adoption of a carbon fee.
As noted by the CLC, the Council&#146;s bipartisan plan could cut U.S. CO&acirc;,, emissions in half by 2035, as compared to 2005 levels. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">As the world
increasingly focuses on addressing the biggest emissions challenges, a price on carbon is an important policy component that can aid in solving these challenges. We look forward to continuing our work with stakeholders to help move an effective
policy solution forward. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Read more about ExxonMobil&#146;s climate strategy and our engagements on related policies in our Energy&nbsp;&amp; Carbon
Summary. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><I>Darren Woods is ExxonMobil&#146;s chairman and chief executive officer </I></P>

Petroleum Institute (API) to support a carbon price will contribute to advancing a lower-carbon future. For some time, we have been encouraging trade associations to support a price on carbon and promote actions that enable the goals of the Paris
Agreement. We encourage Congress to adopt this market-based, national policy solution. To better understand the steps needed to help realize a lower-carbon future, it&#146;s helpful to begin with some important context: the world&#146;s growing need
for energy. Underpinning future energy demand is economic growth driven by an increasing population and growing prosperity. The continuing demand for our products is concentrated in three growing sectors: power generation, industrial, and commercial
transportation. Today, there is a lack of lower-emission alternatives to adequately meet the many needs in these sectors, which together account for 80 percent of global energy-related CO2 emissions. We have spent decades researching new
technologies to address this challenge, and recently created a new business, ExxonMobil Low Carbon Solutions, to commercialize key technologies from our extensive research and development portfolio. For example, ExxonMobil Low Carbon Solutions is
advancing plans for more than 20 new carbon capture and storage (CCS) opportunities around the world to enable large-scale emission reductions, building upon a Carbon Capture Venture we began in 2018. We&#146;re the global leader in carbon capture
and have captured more than 40 percent of all the world&#146;s captured anthropogenic CO2. ExxonMobil was the first company in the world to capture more than 120 million tonnes of CO2, equivalent to the annual emissions of more than 25 million cars.
We&#146;ll also leverage ExxonMobil&#146;s significant experience in the production of hydrogen which, when coupled with CCS, is likely to play a critical role in a lower-carbon energy system. Other low-carbon technology focus areas in our portfolio
will be added as they mature to commercialization. We&#146;re planning to invest $3 billion on lower-emission energy solutions through 2025 on top of $10 billion we&#146;ve spent over the past two decades. In other emission-reduction efforts, we are
working to find new and better ways to monitor and reduce methane emissions, including via a collaboration with universities, environmental groups, and other industry partners. In 2019, we reduced our methane emissions nearly 18 percent across our
U.S. unconventional operations, compared to 2016, when the Paris Agreement was signed. Overall, our operated greenhouse gas emissions went down by 6 percent from 2016 to 2019, and we&#146;ve laid out plans for further reductions by 2025. While we
will continue to advance these plans, there is also a clear role for government &#150; which brings us back to policy. Durable, predictable, and cost-effective policies will be required to develop and deploy multiple needed low-carbon technologies
at scale. Carbon pricing would send a clear signal through the market, creating incentives to reduce emissions. Such a signal will also incentivize and coordinate the research, investment, and technology development that is needed to bring about key
solutions. Putting a price on carbon will allow policymakers to eliminate the inefficient patchwork of regulations that is broadly recognized to be more expensive. Through the current approach, policymakers are putting a very high, but hidden, price
on carbon that people can&#146;t see and are unaware they are paying. An explicit price on carbon would be transparent, incentivize behavior to reduce emissions, allow the market to function efficiently, and stimulate the cross-sector opportunities
needed to uncover the largest emission reduction opportunities at the lowest cost. API now joins major trade groups representing diverse industries in publicly backing carbon pricing. Collectively, they echo a report released last August in which
the International Energy Agency noted, &#147;Carbon pricing, in particular, is a useful tool to guide investment decisions, especially those that will have long-term impacts on future emissions.&#148; The Climate Leadership Council (CLC), of which
ExxonMobil is a founding member, is similarly calling for the adoption of a carbon fee. As noted by the CLC, the Council&#146;s bipartisan plan could cut U.S. CO2 emissions in half by 2035, as compared to 2005 levels. As the world increasingly
focuses on addressing the biggest emissions challenges, a price on carbon is an important policy component that can aid in solving these challenges. We look forward to continuing our work with stakeholders to help move an effective policy solution
forward. Read more about ExxonMobil&#146;s climate strategy and our engagements on related policies in our Energy &amp; Carbon Summary. Darren Woods is ExxonMobil&#146;s Chairman and CEO. TAGS CARBON TAX CCS ENERGY &amp; CARBON SUMMARY PARIS
