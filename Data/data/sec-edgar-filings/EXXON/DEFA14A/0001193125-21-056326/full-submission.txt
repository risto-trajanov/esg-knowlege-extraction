<Center><DIV STYLE="width:8.5in" align="left">
 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><I>The following communication may be featured from time to time in various corporate communications and
social media channels. </I></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman" ALIGN="center"><B><I>Introducing Dhaval Bhandari </I></B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B>Energy Factor Hero Image </B></P> <P STYLE="font-size:12pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">


<IMG SRC="g44784g27s05.jpg" ALT="LOGO">
 </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">I grew up more attuned to the world&#146;s energy than the average child. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">My father worked for India&#146;s national oil company, which means I&#146;ve spent a lifetime immersed in the oil and gas sector. At teatime, in addition to
talking about school, our family also discussed how geopolitical forces affect global oil markets. Our neighbors were our father&#146;s coworkers. And our refrigerator boasted the company logo: &#147;Oil flows, nation grows.&#148; </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">To me, energy was everything &#133; and it still is. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Following
in my father&#146;s footsteps led me to becoming a chemical engineer and, eventually, securing a spot with ExxonMobil, where I have a role in helping shape the energy landscape for generations to come. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">But before all that, I spent my childhood in India&#146;s most populous cities, including Chennai, Delhi and Mumbai, where, despite the cosmopolitan setting,
the threat of an energy shortage loomed often. At the time, power shortages were frequent, teaching us not to take for granted that the <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">air-conditioning</FONT> would turn on or the refrigerator would stay cold. My
experiences crystalized the importance of energy security. </P> <P STYLE="font-size:12pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">


<IMG SRC="g44784g61k30.jpg" ALT="LOGO">
 </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><I>Caption: Sitting atop the family car in Chennai at age 7, awaiting one of our weekend family drives. </I></P>
</DIV></Center>


<p style="margin-top:1em; margin-bottom:0em; page-break-before:always">
<HR SIZE="3" style="COLOR:#999999" WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">


<Center><DIV STYLE="width:8.5in" align="left">
 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Access to safe, reliable energy is critical to societal and economic development. That was something my
father understood, and a lesson he passed on to me. As a <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap"><FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">35-year-old</FONT></FONT> researcher at ExxonMobil, I&#146;m working on projects that couldn&#146;t have existed during his
career, but I still prioritize that same goal of improving energy security. </P> <P STYLE="font-size:12pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">


<IMG SRC="g44784g07x95.jpg" ALT="LOGO">
 </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><I>Caption: My family and I visiting Vidhana Soudha, the state legislature building in Bangalore. </I></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B>A future in transition </B></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Of course, today&#146;s energy
challenges are far from what my father could have imagined. We are in the midst of a major transition, exchanging carbon-intensive fuels like coal for a lower-carbon mix of renewables and natural gas. As a young scientist, I take this transition
very seriously. It&#146;s what keeps me up at night, and is why I got my PhD in chemical engineering from Georgia Tech. </P> <P STYLE="font-size:12pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">


<IMG SRC="g44784g07v17.jpg" ALT="LOGO">
 </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><I>Caption: My parents and I at my PhD graduation ceremony at Georgia Tech in 2010. This is particularly poignant because my
father is also a chemical engineer, and he encouraged me to pursue graduate studies. </I></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Here&#146;s what we face. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Billions of consumers are joining the global middle class. I often hear from family back home in India celebrating the purchase of a new car or a bigger home.
Of course, to fuel their cars, power their homes and jump-start economic enterprise, they will need energy. There is an urgency to global energy demand, and it&#146;s our job to meet it. </P>
</DIV></Center>


<p style="margin-top:1em; margin-bottom:0em; page-break-before:always">
<HR SIZE="3" style="COLOR:#999999" WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">


<Center><DIV STYLE="width:8.5in" align="left">
 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">However, as I envision the energy future, I also see an opportunity to satisfy those needs with lower-carbon
energy. For comparison, I look to cell phones as a source of inspiration. Most cell phone owners in India and many other countries never owned a landline; they jumped right into mobile use. I&#146;m hopeful the same thing could happen for developing
nations that are building new energy infrastructures, prioritizing the use of lower-emission energy sources like natural gas and renewables. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">With these
advancements in lower-carbon supplies, we can meet the dual challenge of providing people around the world with reliable energy and fewer emissions. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">All
it takes is scientific ingenuity and a lot of research. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B>Doing more with less </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">That&#146;s where I come in. My role in ExxonMobil Research and Engineering (EMRE) is to help progress groundbreaking technologies and initiatives aimed at
meeting that dual challenge. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">One particularly important piece of the puzzle, my area of expertise, is advanced separation. Right now, refineries separate
the different components of crude oil, like lubricants and gasoline, through a process called distillation &#150; essentially boiling hydrocarbons. That process involves massive amounts of heat, making it energy intensive. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">But what if refineries could separate products without heat? </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">I
worked with a team of experts from ExxonMobil, my alma mater Georgia Tech and the Imperial College London to answer that question. Our team developed a new <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">non-phase</FONT> change approach using filtration.
Specifically, we developed membranes that, under the right amount of pressure, could filter at the molecular level at room temperature. When scaled to industrial levels, the technology could significantly reduce ExxonMobil&#146;s energy consumption
and emissions &#150; and, in the process, shrink the carbon footprint of everyday products like gasoline and components used to make plastics. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">And
that&#146;s just the start. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman"><B>Introducing energy 2.0 </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">Looking ahead 20 years, I truly believe the membrane technology we&#146;re using today to process oil and gas could also be a key enabler to carbon capture and
storage, a process called CCS. I became passionate about this research topic early in my career, even before coming to ExxonMobil. At age 26, I worked at General Electric Global Research and became one of the youngest principal investigators for a
team of researchers for a U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) grant. My work explored the use of ultra-thin filters to significantly reduce CO2 emissions from coal-fired power plants and yielded promising results in the lab that inspire my thinking
today. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">The challenge is that, to really make an impact, these technologies need to scale up to real-world use. Refineries won&#146;t accept a technology
just because it&#146;s new and sophisticated; it needs to be safe, reliable and thoroughly tested. For scientists, this is when the fun begins. But this is a decade-long process of scaling from a concept in a lab to a robust commercial tool. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:Times New Roman">For most people my age, it&#146;s tough to think in multiple years, rather than weeks or months. But I often look back just a generation ago to the late
1970s, when scientists began scaling up a new, ambitious method of desalination. Rather than boil sea water to rid it of salt, they created a cutting-edge membrane filter. Many critics derided the new technology, saying it would never work. And yet
today, companies are building energy-efficient desalination plants able to filter a record 900,000 cubic meters of water (that&#146;s 240<U></U>&nbsp;million gallons) daily using the reverse osmosis membrane technology. Though it took time to scale
the idea, we&#146;re seeing an impact that was once unimaginable. </P>
