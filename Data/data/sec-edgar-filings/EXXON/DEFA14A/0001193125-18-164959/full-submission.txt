</TABLE> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL"><B>Re: Supplemental Information Related to Item 3 &#150; Advisory Vote to Approve Executive Compensation </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">Dear Investor, </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">As you may have noted, Institutional Shareholder Services (ISS) has
issued a voting recommendation inconsistent with the Board&#146;s recommendation on the <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap"><FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">Say-on-Pay</FONT></FONT> proposal for ExxonMobil&#146;s upcoming annual meeting. We disagree
with their assessment of our executive compensation program. ExxonMobil&#146;s compensation program effectively aligns executive pay with Company performance and the interests and experience of our long-term shareholders, as highlighted in our 2018
Proxy and <I>Executive Compensation Overview</I>. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">The ISS analysis is underpinned by a philosophy that drives toward similar pay programs for companies in
different industries and with different business models. This issue is compounded by flawed and incomplete analysis. We believe their recommendations are not in our shareholders&#146; interest and, if implemented, would weaken the Company and
undermine shareholder value. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">Below are the highlights of our concerns, and <B>Attachment 1</B> provides additional detail on each concern. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">Specifically, we disagree with the underlying basis of the ISS recommendation for the following reasons: </P>
<P STYLE="font-size:6pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P>

<TD WIDTH="3%" VALIGN="top" ALIGN="left">&#149;</TD>
<TD WIDTH="1%" VALIGN="top">&nbsp;</TD>
<TD ALIGN="left" VALIGN="top"><B>ISS&#146; quantitative screening process is flawed:</B> </TD></TR></TABLE> <P STYLE="font-size:6pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P>
<TABLE STYLE="BORDER-COLLAPSE:COLLAPSE; font-family:ARIAL; font-size:10pt" BORDER="0" CELLPADDING="0" CELLSPACING="0" WIDTH="100%">
<TR style = "page-break-inside:avoid">
<TD WIDTH="4%">&nbsp;</TD>

 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL"><B>Attachment 1 &#150; Details on Specific Issues with ISS&#146; Proxy Analysis </B></P>
<P STYLE="font-size:12pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P>

<TD ALIGN="left" VALIGN="top"><B>Quantitative Screen Results </B></TD></TR></TABLE> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">We have the following concerns with the results of ISS&#146; quantitative screen:
</P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL"><B>Peer Group </B></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">As we noted in correspondence with ISS on March&nbsp;5,
2018, it is difficult to understand how Berkshire Hathaway (Berkshire) was selected by ISS as a benchmark company for the most recent assessment period, given the substantially different profiles of the two businesses. We did not receive a response
from ISS to our March&nbsp;5 inquiry seeking clarification on this matter. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">All other companies in the <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">ISS-selected</FONT> peer
group are companies that are capital-intensive, have engineering or manufacturing profiles, and/or long investment lead times. Regardless of its size, Berkshire is a holding company for unrelated businesses, many of which have business models that
are finance or insurance related and do not align with that of ExxonMobil. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">Additionally, we would expect to see similar companies selected by ISS as benchmark
companies for ExxonMobil and our largest U.S.-based competitor in the oil/gas industry. Yet, Berkshire was not selected by ISS as a benchmark for ExxonMobil&#146;s largest U.S.-based competitor or any other company, according to our analysis. Also,
the <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">ISS-selected</FONT> peer group for Berkshire does not include ExxonMobil, nor does it include our largest U.S.-based competitor. Appropriately, most of Berkshire&#146;s
<FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">ISS-selected</FONT> benchmark group includes financial services and insurance companies. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">Furthermore, based on our analysis, the
utilization of companies more comparable in size, capital investment profiles, and business models would result in a more positive score. Also, based on our analysis, we believe that utilization of the
<FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">ISS-selected</FONT> benchmark companies for our largest U.S.-based competitor would also yield a more favorable outcome in the quantitative analysis for ExxonMobil. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">Finally, it is impossible to accurately measure market orientation for pay when the ISS selected benchmark companies change substantially on an annual basis. In the
last 4 years, there have been 15 changes to the <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">ISS-selected</FONT> peer group for ExxonMobil. During the same period ExxonMobil&#146;s Compensation Committee changed only 2 benchmark companies (they removed
Caterpillar and added General Motors in 2017 to ensure that the peer group continues to reflect companies of sufficient size, scale, and complexity for comparison). </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:18pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL"><B>Relative TSR Performance </B></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">While we agree with ISS that relative TSR
performance is one of several appropriate metrics on which to base company performance, comparing ExxonMobil&#146;s relative TSR to companies outside of the oil/gas industry is not meaningful for the purpose of determining relative performance
underlying pay decisions. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">A cross-industry TSR comparison, especially against companies and industries that are not exposed to commodity cycles or have different
(or sometimes opposite) business cycles, does not accurately portray ExxonMobil&#146;s relative TSR performance. We believe it is more accurate and informative to compare TSR across companies of similar size, scale, and complexity in the same
industry. This is the basis on which the Compensation Committee measures TSR and other financial and operating metrics for the purpose of determining executive compensation (see our 2018 Proxy, pages <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">34-35).</FONT>
</P>

 <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL"><B>Benchmark Level of Compensation </B></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">The ISS model relies on a simple comparison of CEO compensation based on the reported pay from the Summary Compensation Table. We do not agree that using reported pay to
determine the relative position of CEO compensation across benchmark companies provides a complete view of the total pay package. A more complete analysis, and one that illustrates whether pay is aligned with changes in company performance, would
include compensation actually realized and paid out in combination with the market value of unrealized compensation (the market value of compensation granted, but not paid out yet). As illustrated in our 2018 Proxy, chart 4, page 33, the market
orientation for the CEO position is at the 42<SUP STYLE="font-size:85%; vertical-align:top">nd</SUP> percentile when using this methodology (or the 44<SUP STYLE="font-size:85%; vertical-align:top">th</SUP> percentile if including 2008-2017
compensation per the May&nbsp;2, 2018 Supplemental Disclosure, chart 4, page 1), as compared with the 71<SUP STYLE="font-size:85%; vertical-align:top">st</SUP> percentile in the ISS quantitative model. This &#147;realized and unrealized&#148;
analysis, over the most recent <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">10-year</FONT> period, better reflects the intent of the Compensation Committee when granting annual compensation to the CEO as it relates to managing the long-term market orientation
of our CEO position. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">Further, the ISS analysis does not fully recognize the significant (36 percent) reduction in total pay to the CEO position upon the transition
to the new incumbent in 2017, nor does it recognize the 50 percent reduction in the bonus program over the last 5 years. Additionally, share grants were reduced for the CEO and Named Executive Officers. </P>
<P STYLE="font-size:18pt;margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt">&nbsp;</P>

<TD WIDTH="4%" VALIGN="top" ALIGN="left"><B>2.</B></TD>
<TD ALIGN="left" VALIGN="top"><B>Performance Criteria at Grant for stock-based LTI program: </B></TD></TR></TABLE> <P STYLE="margin-top:6pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">ISS is effectively recommending a <FONT
STYLE="white-space:nowrap">3-year</FONT> target-setting methodology for the stock program (LTI) in a business with <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">10-year</FONT> investment lead times. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">We believe that ISS should focus their assessment on the question of whether the compensation program is performance-based, versus assessing the method of achieving a
performance-based outcome, which should be unique to each industry and company business model. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">The reduction in share grant to our CEO in 2017, even though the
incumbent was promoted from President to CEO, contributed to an overall pay reduction for the CEO position of 36&nbsp;percent. This underscores the performance basis of the program. At the same time, the methodology for achieving this outcome is
aligned with the business model. This decision reflects the Compensation Committee&#146;s belief that the stock-based LTI program does not have to be based on short-term target-setting to be performance-based. </P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">In the performance share program, assessing performance at grant is the lynchpin for the most important features of our long-term program: 1) the ability to have a
performance period (10 years) that aligns with the investment lead times of our business, and 2) restriction periods on performance shares (10+ years) that are 3 times longer than those found in most industries. We have received direct feedback from
shareholders who support this design feature, especially from those required to hold our shares long-term (e.g., passive fund investors). </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">If we changed the program
to apply performance criteria at vest, it would require implementing a program that involves short-term target-setting (3 years or less) to maintain <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap"><FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">line-of-sight</FONT></FONT> so
that credible and achievable performance targets could be established. This time frame does not align with our business model, and would incentivize executives to take a short-term view in making business decisions. This approach would be
inconsistent with the realities of our business, in which capital projects can take decades to show results. </P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL">The risk of senior executive attrition also needs to
be considered. Changing the stock-based LTI program to incorporate shorter restriction periods and faster monetization of awards increases the risk of attrition among executives at all levels. This risk is compounded by the scale and scope of our
business, which makes the Company a frequent target of recruiters for <FONT STYLE="white-space:nowrap">mid-size</FONT> oil/gas companies. The long restriction periods in the current compensation program create a significant barrier to exit for
executives through a buyout hurdle composed of unvested shares. Any reduction in this buyout hurdle and resulting executive attrition could undermine the long range business plans of the Company. </P>
 <p STYLE="margin-top:0pt;margin-bottom:0pt ; font-size:8pt">&nbsp;</P> <P STYLE="margin-top:0pt; margin-bottom:0pt; font-size:10pt; font-family:ARIAL" ALIGN="right">2 </P>
